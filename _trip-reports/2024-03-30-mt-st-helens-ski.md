title: Mt St Helens Ski
---


We arrived a little before 8:30 and were able to get a spot near the trailhead, despite people trying to save spots for their party members. I slept in my car and my new quilt was nice and warm, mark slept in his tent which was apparently loud from people. We agreed to wake up at 3, but there was activity going on as early as 2 so we both were awake by then. With all of the faffing to get ready we got going at almost exactly 4am, right on schedule. 

Driving through cougar there was basically no snow, we checked the cougar sno park and saw that you could drive up past it towards LRR, so we were worried about being able to ski to and from the trailhead. Luckily there was (barely) enough snow to skin from the car and we made good time heading up the trail, passing a good number of people and being passed by 1 or 2. We made it to chocolate falls at 5. At 4,700' the trail was hemmed in by rocks and too steep so we put our skis on our packs and boot packed. 

We kept making good progress and were above the weather station (~6,100') as the sun rose from behind Adams. We kept making good progress in a strong boot pack with well developed steps. I got into a good rhythm and was able to go consistently relatively quickly and passed a few people. At  7,700', Mark transitioned back to skinning and I bootpacked a few dozen feet then transitioned as well as I was clearly slower than Mark and my back was hurting from the skis. Around this point we also started seeing windblown ice formations which only got larger and more common towards the rim. 

Once we transitioned we were making vertical progress faster than the hikers despite the switchbacks but I felt a lot worse and had to take a few breaks. I am guessing this is because with skinning there isn't the break every step (breathe, step, breathe, step...) or something else with the different motion. I ate some and felt slightly better and we were able to make it within sight of the rim and where people were congregating. 

We also saw the boot path towards the true summit and aimed to catch just below it, cutting the corner and missing the congregating area. That path only went a short distance before tapering out so we confirmed with our navigation and kept going towards the true summit on pristine snow. We ended up going down about 200' to give ample space for the cornice and to pass a gulley. After we started making upward progress we spotted another skier coming towards us, having cut from monitor ridge lower than we did. We made it  the 300' back up to what we determined was the summit and were careful to stay back from the cornice. 

We made it to the summit just after 9am and sat for about 40 minutes to eat lunch, wait for the other skiers (another party of 2 joined us after the solo skier), take photos, and transition to downhill. 

We set off around 9:45 and initially traversed as high as we could (still below our skin track over so well away from the cornice) back to the climbing route. We then followed near the climbing route down as many hikers came up the main path. Consulting with our navigation and the terrain and monitoring the snow condition (more on that later), we went down in many different pitches, each time agreeing to meet at a certain landmark. For a few pitches that were slightly steeper we went one at a time out of caution but the snow was stable for us. We also were near another pair of skiers and would either go before them or let them go for each pitch, monitoring how it seemed for them and incorporating that into our plans, but still setting our own route. 

As we went down, we noticed how aspect dependent the snow quality was. On west facing (shaded) aspects, the snow had a crust of ice and on east facing (sunny) aspects the snow was smooth and soft. On the soft faces we did not see any rollerballs or trigger any sloughs but it was clear that it was heading that way as it warmed up (and an observation submitted to NWAC later in the day confirmed this, with a small wet loose avy on a slope we saw). I am glad we left as early as we did as I would not want to be anywhere in the sun after when we went through. 

We then followed the skin track out through the trees which was rather narrow and felt hard to control at times, but we were able to use the snow on either side to slow down or pass climbers ascending and descending and were never actually out of control. The last half mile or so was such a slight incline that we had to duck walk and use our poles for any small incline but we still made it to the parking lot between 10:45 and 11:00am, for an hour of descending. 

Overall time split was:

~5 hours ascending 

~45 minutes at the top

~1 hour descending

<7 hours car to car 

There were cars parked on both sides of the road for a long ways leading up to the Marble Mountain Sno-Park, maybe 1/3 of the way to the Cougar Sno-Park. On the way out of town we drove up the road towards LRR, about 1/3 of the way before it turned to snow and there were some trucks with trailers parked there (snowmobilers?). We also stopped at the Trail of Two Forests to show Mark the entrance to Lape cave and the interpretive trail with cool tree casts. The trail was completely snow free, unlike when we were there two weeks prior. 

We stopped for fast food in Kelso and returned to Seattle by 4pm. Total round trip time for Mark was under 24 hours, just over for me.
